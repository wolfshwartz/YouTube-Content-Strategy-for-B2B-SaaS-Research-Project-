## Research: YouTube Content Strategy for B2B SaaS

I choose this topic from my genuine interest in B2B YouTube strategy as a niche. I see a growth and opportunity for every B2B founders who want to do a branding and grow their reputation by using Youtube. For me, Youtube provides both short form and long form content which suits for SaaS business who want to walkthrough their products or even do short content to gain attraction which exceeds other social platform performance in terms of visualization. This topic turned out to be a live active space right now with real named experts producing recent specific content rather than recycled agency listicles.

I found and reviewed 10 experts who actively build content strategy. Some of them provide exact Youtube content strategy for B2B SaaS, but some others are more broad on content strategy that specifying about growing Youtube channels and gain leads which still applicable for B2B SaaS to grow their Youtube channel. See `/research/sources.md` for the full list with links and annotations.

Collected 12 YouTube transcripts and 6 LinkedIn posts, organized by author in `/research/youtube-transcripts/` and `/research/linkedin-posts/`. Where no matching LinkedIn content existed, that's documented in `/research/other/`.

Key patterns I noticed across sources:

- **Treat YouTube as a search engine, not a broadcast channel.** The strongest-performing SaaS channels (Samu Kovács' Calendly teardown, Greg Miroslaw's Instantly vs. Lemlist comparison, Marlon Doll's GitHub case study, Sam Oh's Ahrefs SEO breakdown) all start from keyword/topic research on what buyers are actually searching for — not what the company wants to announce.
- **The same funnel structure shows up almost everywhere, just with different names.** Top-of-funnel (broad, non-branded, educational) → middle-of-funnel (bridge/nurture: case studies, comparisons) → bottom-of-funnel (product-specific, conversion) is essentially identical across Samu Kovács' "40/30/30 split," Greg Miroslaw's funnel breakdown, and Marlon Doll's "Authority / Bridge / Conversion" framework.
- **The single most-repeated mistake is leading with product features instead of the customer's problem.** Calling out the product name in the title, or posting "new feature" videos, was flagged independently by multiple sources as why B2B SaaS channels stay stuck at a few hundred views.
- **Packaging (title + thumbnail) is treated as the highest-leverage lever for clicks** — repeatedly described as more important than the video itself, since its only job is to stop the scroll and get the title read.
- **Consistency beats one-off virality.** Several sources gave a specific commitment threshold (e.g. one video/week for at least 90 days) before judging whether a strategy is working.
- **Genuine practitioners back claims with specific, checkable numbers** (view counts, revenue, subscriber growth) rather than vague claims — this held up in practice as the clearest signal separating real practitioners from generic advice-givers.
